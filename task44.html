Section 4.4 — Sensitivity Analysis

Effect of Propellant Mass

Vary the propellant load from 10 to 90 kg with fixed thrust and payload. Investigate how the initial acceleration profile, burnout altitude and maximum apogee respond to the trade-off between lighter rocket and longer burn.

m_prop = 10 / 50 / 90 kg T = 1500 N fixed Payload MP = 10 kg · ISA
📋
Exercise Statement
Section 4.4 of the project brief

Vary the propellant mass: m_prop = 10 kg, 50 kg and 90 kg, keeping the payload mass MP = 10 kg and the engine thrust T = 1500 N constant. The mass flow rate ṁ = T/(g₀·Isp) is the same for all cases since thrust and Isp are fixed.

Q1How does the acceleration profile change with propellant mass? Compare the initial net acceleration a₀ = T/m₀ − g₀ for each case and explain the shape of the a(t) curve.
Q2How does the burnout altitude change? Is it always the case that more propellant → higher maximum altitude?
⚙️

Trade-off: less propellant → lighter rocket → higher initial acceleration. But more propellant → longer burn → higher burnout altitude and more total delta-v. After burnout the rocket coasts ballistically — the combined effect determines which case reaches the highest apogee.

Analysis
Answers derived from simulation
Q1 — Acceleration profile
a₀ = T/m₀ − g₀: lighter rocket (10 kg prop) starts at 65.2 m/s² vs 5.2 m/s² for 90 kg. However, all three curves show the same shape: a(t) increases during the burn as mass decreases (m decreases → T/m increases → a increases), then drops sharply to gravitational deceleration at burnout. The 10 kg case reaches very high peak acceleration but burns out at only 972 m.
Q2 — Burnout altitude and maximum apogee
More propellant always wins: z_max = 1.48 / 6.48 / 11.28 km for 10 / 50 / 90 kg. Even though the 10 kg rocket has 12× higher initial acceleration, it exhausts its fuel in 6.5 s at only 972 m. The 90 kg rocket burns for 58.8 s reaching 9916 m at burnout, then coasts to 11.28 km. Total impulse (= ṁ·g₀·Isp·t_burn = T·t_burn) scales directly with propellant mass — more propellant, more total delta-v.

"""
Task 4.4 — Propellant Mass Variation
m_prop = 10 / 50 / 90 kg · Fixed T = 1500 N · Payload MP = 10 kg
"""
import numpy as np
from rocket_simulation import simulate_rocket, compute_metrics, print_metrics, T0, ISP, MP, G0
from plot_utils import plot_comparison

PROP_MASSES = [10.0, 50.0, 90.0]
LABELS      = ['m_prop = 10 kg', 'm_prop = 50 kg', 'm_prop = 90 kg']
COLORS      = ['#5B9BD5', '#2A6496', '#0D2B55']
MDOT = T0 / (G0 * ISP)   # 1.530 kg/s — same for all cases

results, metrics_list = [], []
for mp in PROP_MASSES:
    m0 = MP + mp; tb = mp/MDOT; a0 = T0/m0 - G0
    print(f"m_prop={mp:.0f}kg  m0={m0:.0f}kg  t_burn={tb:.2f}s  a0={a0:.2f}m/s²")
    t, r, v, drag, mass, accel = simulate_rocket(m_propellant=mp)
    results.append((t, r, v, drag, mass, accel))
    metrics_list.append(compute_metrics(t, r, v))

# Q2 — burnout altitudes
for mp, res in zip(PROP_MASSES, results):
    t_arr, r_arr = res[0], res[1]; tb = mp/MDOT
    idx = int(np.argmin(np.abs(t_arr - tb)))
    print(f"m_prop={mp:.0f}kg  z_burn={r_arr[idx,2]:.0f}m  z_max={metrics_list[...]['z_max']/1000:.2f}km")

# Interactive 4-panel plot: altitude, speed, mass, acceleration
# Burnout marked with dotted vertical lines
plot_comparison(panels=[...], labels=LABELS, colors=COLORS,
                suptitle='Task 4.4 — Propellant Mass Variation')
